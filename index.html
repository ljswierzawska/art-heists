Part I

A perfect crime in numbers

Of the estimated 50,000 works of art stolen every year, only about one in ten will ever be seen again. The rest vanish, absorbed into private collections, smuggled across borders, or simply hidden away, sometimes for decades. Recovery almost never happens by accident. It takes tipoffs, undercover operations, or a thief trying to sell a painting so famous that every dealer in the world knows to call the police. For the other 90%, the trail goes cold fast.

Art theft, sometimes called artnapping, sounds like the perfect crime: low odds of getting caught, high-value targets often easy to snatch. Still, it’s a tricky business to get into. On the black market, a work’s value is hard to realize. To make it pay, thieves often need a buyer lined up in advance. More often, stolen art ends up as collateral or leverage in ransom demands.

Part II

Lost and found

If uncovered, art crimes can land a thief up to 10 years in prison or result in heavy fines. They can also make for some of the most cinematic crimes. Among the 113 heists featured in the interactive infographic "Lost but not forgotten: A look at famous art heists around the world", cases are almost evenly split between those solved and unsolved.

Part III

The most stolen artist in the world

Monet. Picasso. Rembrandt. The most stolen artists read like a museum wall of fame, which is, of course, exactly the point. Thieves don't steal obscure work. They steal names, because names carry value that transcends any single painting. A Picasso is recognizable to someone who has never set foot in a gallery. But it's Monet that has stolen thieves' hearts most. Of the 113 famous art heists, the French Impressionist was a part of 11. Art thieves' favorite destination? Paris, France. The art mecca experienced 17 of the major heists.
