Part I

A perfect crime in numbers

Of the estimated 50,000 works of art stolen every year, only about one in ten will ever be seen again. The rest vanish, absorbed into private collections, smuggled across borders, or simply hidden away, sometimes for decades. Recovery almost never happens by accident. It takes tipoffs, undercover operations, or a thief trying to sell a painting so famous that every dealer in the world knows to call the police. For the other 90%, the trail goes cold fast.

Part II

The most stolen artist in the world

Monet. Picasso. Rembrandt. The most stolen artists read like a museum wall of fame, which is, of course, exactly the point. Thieves don't steal obscure work. They steal names, because names carry value that transcends any single painting. A Picasso is recognizable to someone who has never set foot in a gallery. That recognition is the whole game. The irony is that it also makes these works nearly impossible to sell openly, which is why so many end up used as collateral in criminal deals, or locked in a crate somewhere, unseen by anyone. Stolen not to be enjoyed — just to be owned.

Rembrandt — already a tragic figure who died broke — has had more than his share of posthumous humiliation. His works have been stolen, lost, cut up, and forged across centuries. The Dutch master's dark palette and intimate portraits make them among the most recognizable works in Western art.

Which brings us to Boston. March 1990. The greatest art heist in history — and the one that has never been solved.
